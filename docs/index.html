Your brain cannot run backpropagation.

Backprop is the algorithm behind every neural network you have ever heard of. It is also, physically, something a synapse cannot do — for reasons we can name precisely. So we built the biologically legal alternative, raced them on handwritten digits, and went looking for the exact place where the accuracy comes from.

Test accuracy on MNIST. Same 784→400→10 network in every case. The only difference is how the middle layer learns. Backprop wins by 8.7 points — and the interesting question is which of its biologically impossible moves is actually paying for that.

The question

What we're actually comparing.

A neural network is a stack of numbers. Each connection between two units holds one number — a weight — and the whole network's knowledge is nothing but the pattern of those numbers. Training a network means answering one question, over and over: given what just happened, how should each of these numbers change?

There are two famous answers, and they could not be more different in spirit.

Hebbian, 1949
If the cell sending the signal and the cell receiving it were both active, make the number bigger. That's it. Donald Hebb proposed this with no evidence at all. Twenty-four years later, biologists found the mechanism.
Backprop, 1986
Work out how much this specific number contributed to the network's mistake, and nudge it to make the mistake smaller. This is the credit-assignment problem, and backprop solves it exactly, using calculus.

The bridge — and we have to be honest about it

You cannot run a fair race between these two straight out of the box, and it's worth saying why rather than hiding it.

Hebbian learning is unsupervised. It has no idea what a "7" is. It has never been told the answer to anything. So it cannot classify digits — not because it's weak, but because classification is a question it was never asked. Backprop, by contrast, is defined by being told the answer.

So we do what Fukushima did in 1980, and what the field still does: let the Hebbian rule learn features, then bolt a small supervised classifier on top of those features. To keep it fair, every model on the bench gets the same bolt-on classifier — one linear readout, trained with labels, identical in all four cases.

input 784  ───▶  hidden 400  ───▶  readout 10 The readout is supervised in all four models. The only thing we vary is how the 400 hidden weights are learned. Everything on this page is a claim about that one layer.

Datasets: MNIST (70,000 handwritten digits) and Fashion-MNIST (70,000 clothing photos, same shape, harder). Both are standard public benchmarks. 60,000 train / 10,000 test, 5 random seeds per condition.

The synapse's-eye view

What a synapse can physically see.

A synapse is a junction between two cells. It is a physical object, roughly a micron across, and it has access to exactly three things: the cell before it, the cell after it, and its own current strength. Nothing else in your brain is wired to it.

So: pick one synapse. Black out everything it cannot read. Then ask each learning rule to do its job with what's left.

Learning rule

The objection, in one sentence

To apply backprop's update, a synapse in the hidden layer needs the numerical values of the weights in the output layer — weights belonging to synapses it has never touched and has no wire to. This is the weight transport problem, and it is the sharpest version of "backprop is not biologically plausible."

It is worth being precise here, because the usual slogan overshoots. People often say "the brain has no global error signal." That's too strong — dopamine broadcasts something a lot like one. What the brain plausibly lacks is narrower and more damning: a per-synapse, precisely credited error, and the weight transport needed to compute it.

A synapse in the hidden layer would have to read the values of 4,000 weights it has never touched. There is no wire in the brain that carries them.

Is that copied wire really the expensive part? Lillicrap et al. (2016) replaced it with a fixed random matrix that nobody ever updates — and the network still learned. So weight transport, the most flagrant violation backprop commits, turns out to be the cheap one to give up. What you cannot give up cheaply is the label.

The Hebbian layer we actually built

Plain "fire together, wire together" has a fatal flaw: it's a loop with no brake. Stronger connection → the second cell fires harder → the connection strengthens more → the weights run away to infinity. The rule can only ever say more.

Erkki Oja fixed this in 1982 with a trick that stays local. Grow on coincidence, but subtract a little in proportion to how hard the output cell just fired:

Δw = η · y · ( x − y · w ) Oja's rule. The shrink term hits every synapse on the cell; only the ones that participated earn it back. Growth is now funded by a tax everybody pays — which turns Hebbian learning into a competition. And a single neuron running this converges on the first principal component of whatever it's shown, using nothing but local information.

Our hidden layer is four local rules, and each one is borrowed from a different piece of real neuroscience:

In the codeWhat it doesWhere biology does this
settle units inhibit each other until activity stabilises lateral inhibition in cortex
ΔW Hebbian growth toward the input, Oja-renormalised LTP · NMDA receptors as coincidence detectors
ΔL units that co-fire inhibit each other more Földiák's anti-Hebbian rule (1990)
Δθ each unit's threshold drifts to keep its firing rate near a target synaptic scaling (Turrigiano)

Every one of these uses only pre-synaptic activity, post-synaptic activity, and the synapse's own state. Nothing reaches across the network. That is the entire design constraint.

1943 – 2019

Biology gave machine learning the neuron. Then it stopped returning calls.

The artificial neuron did not begin as an engineering convenience. It began as a claim about the brain — and for its first forty years, the field kept checking its work against neuroscience. Then backprop arrived, worked far too well to argue with, and the two fields drifted apart.

Hebb proposed his rule in 1949 with no experimental evidence whatsoever. The molecular machinery that implements it — an NMDA receptor that only opens when the sending cell has released glutamate and the receiving cell is already depolarised — is a literal coincidence detector. A logical AND gate, built out of protein. Bliss & Lømo found it in 1973.

Four of the papers on this timeline are sitting in our repository — McCulloch & Pitts, Rosenblatt, Hubel & Wiesel, and Fukushima. Fukushima's Neocognitron is the one that matters most for us: it trained its feature layers with unsupervised competition and put a supervised readout on top. We did not invent that bridge. We rebuilt it.

Result 1 · the ablation ladder

Not "who wins." Which illegal move is doing the work.

Everybody knows backprop wins. That result is worth nothing on its own. So instead we built a ladder: start from a layer that cannot learn at all, and add back one piece of biologically impossible machinery at a time. Then watch where the accuracy actually jumps.

Test accuracy · hidden-layer learning rule

Bars are the mean of 5 seeds; whiskers are ±1 standard deviation. The axis starts at 70% — the gaps are the point, and a 0–100 axis would flatten them. Hover or tab through a bar to see what it costs biologically.

Hebbian

Read it from the bottom. A frozen random layer — one that never learns anything — already gets 85.66%, because 400 random projections of a digit are already a decent description of it. That's the bar. Anything that can't beat it hasn't learned a thing.

Our Hebbian layer clears it by 3.4 points, consistently, across every seed. That is a real result: a rule with no labels, no error signal, and no wire longer than a synapse found genuine structure in raw pixels.

Then you add back the global, label-driven error signal — and accuracy jumps ~8.7 points to 97.75%.

A local, label-free rule beats a layer that never learns — by 3.4 points, on every seed. Then handing the network the right answer is worth another 8.7. That second number is the price of the credit-assignment problem, and it is the thing biology still has to explain.

The random control is why the middle bar means anything at all. Without it, 89% is just a number — you would have no idea whether the Hebbian rule found real structure, or whether any 400 random directions would have done just as well.

Result 1b · what the layer learned

The less accurate model is the one you can actually read.

Every hidden unit has 784 incoming weights — one per pixel. So its weights are a 28×28 image. Draw them back out and you can see exactly what that unit decided was worth noticing. This is not a metaphor; it's just the weight matrix, reshaped.

Hidden units, drawn as images
72 of 400 · warm = pixel excites this unit · rose = pixel argues against it

This is the most quietly surprising thing we found. The biologically plausible model is also the interpretable one. Hebbian units converge on recognisable strokes and templates. Backprop's units converge on speckle that means nothing to a human — and outperform them by nearly nine points.

There is a number for this. Of 400 available directions, the Hebbian layer's activity effectively occupies about 108; the random layer's occupies about 347. Random noise sprays across the whole space. The Hebbian layer compressed — which is what finding structure looks like, measured.

Hubel and Wiesel, 1962: cells in the cat's visual cortex fire for oriented edges. Nobody told those cells what an edge was. Local, unsupervised learning on real input produces feature detectors — in a cat, and on our bench.

Result 2 · the credit-assignment gap, made visible

Then we gave it a harder job, and the whole thing collapsed.

Swap MNIST for Fashion-MNIST — same 28×28 images, same network, but photos of clothing instead of digits. Flip the toggle on the chart above and watch what happens to the amber bar.

On Fashion-MNIST, the Hebbian layer scores 73.80% and the untrained random layer scores 74.40%. They are statistically tied. Every point our biologically plausible learner earned on MNIST evaporated.

This is not a bug. It's the deepest result in the project, and it has an exact cause.

A Hebbian rule is shaped by input statistics: it tunes itself to whatever pattern recurs most in what it's shown. Backprop is shaped by the task: its features exist to reduce a specific error on a specific question.

On MNIST those two things happen to coincide. The most common recurring pixel patterns in handwritten digits are the things that tell digits apart, because digits genuinely look different from each other. So a rule that has never heard the word "seven" does fine.

On Fashion-MNIST they come apart. A shirt, a coat, and a pullover share nearly the same silhouette. The most common recurring pattern is the silhouette — which is precisely the information that does not separate the classes. So the Hebbian layer dutifully learns the same generic garment blob over and over, and hands the readout 400 near-duplicate features. You can see it directly in the learned filters: on MNIST every unit is a distinct stroke; on Fashion-MNIST they collapse into a handful of shared shapes.

Features shaped by the input versus features shaped by the task. That is the credit-assignment problem, and this is what it costs you — not in theory, in points.

A second bill, that nobody mentions

There's a knob on the Hebbian layer that sets how many units are allowed to fire at once — its sparsity. We tuned it. It helped on MNIST and hurt on Fashion-MNIST.

Same tuning, opposite signs
target rate 0.08 → 0.20

There is no universal right answer for that knob — the correct sparsity is task-dependent. And a Hebbian layer has no way to find it, because it has no task signal to learn from. A human has to guess, per dataset.

Backprop's gradient just… handles this. It adapts to whatever the task needs, automatically, because that is literally what a gradient is.

So biological plausibility costs you twice: once in accuracy, and once again in hand-tuning that the gradient would have done for you.

Result 3 · the one where we were wrong

We predicted the brain-like model would forget less. It doesn't.

Neural networks have a notorious failure mode: teach one a new thing and it obliterates the old thing. It's called catastrophic forgetting, and humans obviously don't do it. We thought a local, sparse, brain-like rule might resist it. So we ran the test.

Split-MNIST: train on digits 0–4. Then train on 5–9. Then go back and ask about 0–4.

Accuracy on digits 0–4, before and after learning 5–9
Split-MNIST · 2 seeds · hover a bar for its value
before — what it knew after — what it can still do after, with a fresh readout — what it still knows

Everything forgets. Backprop drops from 98.93% to 0.63%. Hebbian drops from 97.57% to 1.90% — very slightly better, and nowhere near enough to matter.

We had a hypothesis for why Hebbian might hold up: sparsity. If old digits and new digits light up mostly different neurons, they can't overwrite each other. So we ran sparse and dense versions of both models to separate "the learning rule" from "the sparsity." Sparsity helped backprop noticeably (it lost 94.55 points instead of 98.30) and barely moved Hebbian. So sparsity isn't the explanation either.

Then the control gave us the actual answer. The random layer — frozen, never trained, structurally incapable of learning or forgetting anything — still "forgot" 92.73 points.

A layer that cannot learn cannot forget. So the forgetting was never happening in the hidden layer at all. It was happening in the readout — the tiny classifier on top, quietly redrawing its decision boundary after seeing nothing but 5s through 9s for a while.

The proof is the third bar. Retrain only the readout on the same, drifted features, and accuracy on 0–4 snaps straight back to 96–98% in every single condition. The representations were never destroyed. They were sitting there the whole time. Nobody was listening to them.

This is our favourite result and it is a negative one. Our hypothesis was wrong, the control we almost didn't bother running is what caught it, and the honest conclusion is that neither locality nor sparsity buys you protection from forgetting. What actually would, according to Complementary Learning Systems theory, is replay — the hippocampus rehearsing old memories to the cortex while you sleep. We didn't build that. Somebody should.

So what

Neither thing on our bench is a brain.

It would be easy to end with "backprop wins, but it's not biological." That's the version of this project we were trying not to write, because it's the least interesting true thing you can say. Here is what we'd actually defend:

Backprop is implausible in specific, nameable ways — and they are not all equally expensive. Lillicrap et al. showed that weight transport, the most flagrant violation, can be thrown out almost for free. What nobody has removed cheaply is the error signal that comes from knowing the right answer — and our 8.7-point gap is a measurement of exactly what that signal is worth.

Our Hebbian model is not the brain either. It's a two-term update rule. Real synapses are Hebbian-ish machinery wrapped in a great deal of stabilising and gating apparatus we threw away — spike timing (STDP), neuromodulators like dopamine deciding whether a moment was even worth learning from, and homeostasis on timescales of hours. What's biologically real isn't Oja's formula. It's the constraint Oja's formula respects: grow on coincidence, stay bounded, and use only what you can reach.

And the gap we measured is not a law of nature. Krotov and Hopfield (2019) get a Hebbian rule with stronger competition far closer to backprop on MNIST than we did. Our 8.7 points is a measurement of our rule, not a ceiling on local learning.

Where the field is actually going

The interesting move isn't picking a side — it's dissolving the question. Predictive coding (Rao & Ballard 1999) proposes that cortex is constantly predicting its own input and learning from what it got wrong. Whittington and Bogacz showed in 2017 that a predictive coding network, using nothing but local Hebbian plasticity, can approximate backprop.

Which is a strange and wonderful idea: Hebbian hardware, doing backprop's job. And it's already hiding in Oja's rule. Rewrite it as Δwᵢ ∝ y · (xᵢ − ŷᵢ), where ŷᵢ = y·wᵢ is the neuron's attempt to reconstruct its own input, and Oja's rule is Hebbian learning on the prediction error. The bridge was in the 1982 paper the whole time.

Locality is not only a biological constraint

It is tempting to end by saying the brain is the most efficient neural network there is. Be careful — on a narrow task that is simply false, and our own results are the proof. A small network on a microcontroller classifies a handwritten digit in microjoules. A human spends a second of a 20-watt brain doing the same thing, and is enormously less efficient per digit. Silicon won that race a long time ago.

Where the brain is still untouchable is learning. It learns continuously, from few examples, without a teacher, across every sense at once, without catastrophically forgetting what it knew last week — on about 20 watts, roughly a dim lightbulb. That is the machine we are actually trying to explain, and it is not the one we benchmarked.

And here is the part we did not expect. Locality is not only a rule biology imposes on itself — it is an energy constraint, and it is the same constraint. Backprop has to store every activation from the forward pass and then ship a globally computed error back through the network. In real hardware, moving data is where the energy goes; the arithmetic is nearly free by comparison. A local rule touches only the two neurons it sits between: no stored forward pass, no long-range error traffic, nothing to ship anywhere. This is exactly why neuromorphic chips want local learning rules — they are the only kind you can actually run on the chip.

Nor is "we cannot simulate a brain" mainly a shortage of compute. We have the complete wiring diagram of an adult fruit fly and a cubic millimetre of mouse cortex; a human has around 86 billion neurons and something on the order of a hundred trillion synapses, and we have mapped essentially none of it. But even where we do have the wiring, we do not have the learning rules — which is the whole reason this project exists. We had to guess at a rule, and then report honestly that the piece of it we were most confident about did nothing at all.

So our 8.7 points is not only the price of biological plausibility. It is the price of a learning rule you could run on 20 watts — one that never has to store the past or phone home. Whether that is a bargain or a disaster depends entirely on what you are trying to build, and that is an open question, not a rhetorical one.

What we'd fix

  • The lateral inhibition barely does anything. We implemented Földiák's anti-Hebbian rule correctly, but scaling its learning rate 5–25× changed no result — the lateral weights stayed near zero throughout. The two things that actually made the Hebbian layer work were the homeostatic threshold ramp and scaling each update by the unit's own confidence. We are not going to claim lateral inhibition is why this works, because the evidence says it isn't.
  • Seed counts are modest — 2 to 5, on CPU. Fine for a class project, not a paper.
  • STDP and neuromodulation are out of scope, deliberately. Our images have no time dimension, so spike timing has nothing to bite on; and a reward signal would smuggle supervision back into the one layer that's supposed to be label-free.
  • Next: implement Krotov & Hopfield's rule to see how much of the gap is really ours, and add replay to the forgetting experiment to test the one mechanism that might actually work.
